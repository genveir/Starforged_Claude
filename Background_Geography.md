# Geography

## The Blight

The quarantine zone enforced by the cordon navies. Contains two types of world.

**The Old Worlds** are the planets humanity actually built its civilisation on. Cities, industry, shipyards, factory complexes, precision manufacturing infrastructure of a scale and sophistication that dwarfs anything currently operational in the Divide. All of it still there. All of it potentially functional. Surface operations are essentially impossible — touch the ground and you will eventually carry the blight somewhere new.

**The Dead Worlds** are a different kind of tragedy. These are planets people fled to during the exodus, unknowingly carrying the blight with them — a second wave of catastrophe following the first. They have human graves on them, and the ruins of settlements that never stood a chance. The blight didn't find these people. They brought it themselves. This distinction matters culturally: the Old Worlds were taken from humanity by something external and incomprehensible. The Dead Worlds were destroyed by human panic and human error, and that knowledge sits differently.

**The Shell**
The region immediately surrounding the Blight — the destinations people reached during the panicked exodus before the navies concentrated survivors into the Refuge. Almost entirely depopulated now, but not empty. Contains at least three categories of location:

- **Ghost Colonies** — settlements that were abandoned, either because they were unviable, because the navies relocated their populations to the Refuge, or because something went wrong. Range from orderly evacuations where everything useful was stripped out, to places that were simply left mid-occupation. A century of abandonment means anything could still be running — automated systems, AI, infrastructure with no one to oversee it. Ghost Colonies are ready-made exploration locations. Not all of them are entirely dead. Some may have been quietly reoccupied.

- **Rogue Colonies** — settlements that refused navy authority. They don't pay taxes, don't accept navy jurisdiction, and don't receive navy protection. They are not actively hunted — the navies have bigger concerns and making martyrs of independent settlers is bad politics. But they exist entirely outside the systems of mutual obligation that make life in the Refuge function. Some are genuinely free and self-sufficient communities that made a principled choice. Others are desperate, preyed upon, and surviving on the margin. The interesting ones are probably somewhere between. Rogue Colonies may have found and use Old World technology and AI systems, having built relationships with whatever they found running in the Shell.

- **Cordon Navy Bases** - the cordon navies maintain a large number of bases in the Shell. Most are small supply points and sensor posts, the bulk of the cordon navies is in the Refuge, but if needs be, there are true battle-ready forces available in fleet bases across the Shell.

The Shell is where rogue AI is most likely to be encountered. Old World technology was running when colonies were abandoned. A century later some of it still is, doing whatever it was designed to do with no oversight and no off switch. An agricultural AI optimising for a population that no longer exists. A defence system still on alert. A mining operation that has been autonomously expanding for a hundred years. These aren't necessarily malevolent — just persistent, unchecked, and potentially very strange to interact with.

## The Refuge
The food-bearing planets of the Refuge are humanity's greatest asset.

This is where almost all humans live. The product of deliberate navy action in the first generation after the exodus — navies relocated populations from the Shell, and concentrated humanity in a region with the most promise. This is the society the background documents describe.

The concentration process has a direct bearing on the planetary navy factions: a navy that transported a population to the Refuge and settled them has a natural claim to authority over that population, and those populations have a complex relationship with the navy that saved them and subsequently rules them — genuine gratitude layered over genuine resentment of the power asymmetry.

The Refuge is not a single contiguous region in any simple geographic sense. Its shape is determined by passage corridor availability rather than raw distance — FTL travel follows natural spatial phenomena, so two worlds physically close might be navigationally isolated while two distant worlds are well connected. The actual inhabited zone is a series of clusters and corridors rather than a uniform sphere.

Some of the largest bases of the cordon navies lie within the Refuge. These serve as a locus of power, as gathering points for the food tax, and as staging points for expeditions and convoys into the Shell and the Blight. Although these bases lie within the cluster of systems known as the Refuge, none of them are at inhabited planets.
