# Interesting Observations

This file holds interesting observations that come up in conversations. People in the setting would be aware of these if it fits their role, and as a GM it's good to keep in mind that these things are true or could happen.

## Orbital Mechanics and Ship Combat

**The moon-as-shield manoeuvre**
A ship hiding behind a moon from a slower pursuer can maintain its protected position by matching the pursuer's angular velocity around the moon, keeping the moon between them at all times. A faster ship can do this while also gaining altitude, spiralling outward while staying in opposition. However, the angular velocity required to maintain opposition increases with altitude — at 5000 kilometres out, a ship would need to be moving roughly 166 times faster along its arc than a pursuer hugging the surface at 30 kilometres. No realistic speed advantage covers that ratio. The manoeuvre works in the lower part of the spiral but breaks down well before a ship reaches meaningful safety distance. The endgame always involves some exposure window; the manoeuvre makes that window as short and unfavourable as possible, not zero.

**Fuel endurance as a strategic asymmetry**
A purpose-built combat vessel and a long-range courier corvette in a prolonged standoff are not evenly matched over time. Combat vessels are built for in-system operations and burn fuel maintaining orbital positions and pursuit vectors. Courier vessels are built for sustained operations across long distances. In a war of attrition with no resupply, the combat vessel runs dry first. A raider commander who understands this will not chase — they will play whatever leverage cards they hold instead, because chasing is a losing game against a ship with more legs.

**The two-ship problem**
A single ship can be kept in opposition behind a moon indefinitely by a faster ship. Two ships on different vectors cannot both be kept on the opposite side of the same moon simultaneously. A second vessel entering the system and coordinating with the first breaks the geometry entirely — the protected ship must run immediately or be bracketed. This makes the arrival of a second hostile vessel the single most dangerous event for a ship currently sheltering behind a moon, and means any faction sophisticated enough to plan a two-ship operation has a strong tactical answer to the moon-as-shield approach.
