## Orbital Mechanics and Ship Combat

**The moon-as-shield manoeuvre**

**[Certain]** A fast ship sheltering behind a moon is safe as long as it can stay in opposition — keeping the moon between itself and a pursuing ship. A single pursuer cannot force an exposure window against a faster ship that is paying attention.

**[Certain]** Graff's comfort sitting in his current position implies that a sufficiently faster ship can escape a two-ship bracketing scenario. If escape were impossible, no competent captain would accept a position where a second hostile vessel entering the system means certain death.

**[Speculative]** The mechanics of that escape likely involve the faster ship accelerating hard and gaining altitude in the early part of the spiral — overtaking the attacker on the angular velocity while climbing — so that by the time the attacker has an over-the-horizon line of sight, the faster ship is already committed to an outward vector and moving away at speed. At that point the 5000 kilometre safety threshold applies, and a ship already moving away reaches it with less distance to cover than one sitting still. Perfect opposition throughout the manoeuvre is not required; good enough opposition for long enough to build that escape velocity is.

**[Speculative]** Whether this works in practice depends on two undefined variables: the relative acceleration profiles of ship classes in the Divide, and how quickly a missile can close on a ship already moving away from it. Neither has been defined. The manoeuvre is plausible in principle; whether it succeeds for a specific ship against a specific pursuer is an open question.

**Fuel endurance as a strategic asymmetry**
**[Certain]** A purpose-built combat vessel and a long-range courier corvette in a prolonged standoff are not evenly matched over time. Combat vessels are built for in-system operations and burn fuel maintaining orbital positions and pursuit vectors. Courier vessels are built for sustained operations across long distances. In a war of attrition with no resupply, the combat vessel runs dry first. A raider commander who understands this will not chase — they will play whatever leverage cards they hold instead, because chasing is a losing game against a ship with more legs.

---

**The two-ship problem**
**[Speculative]** Two ships on different vectors cannot both be kept on the opposite side of the same moon simultaneously. A second vessel entering the system and coordinating with the first breaks the geometry — the protected ship must commit to an escape vector immediately or risk being bracketed. However, see the moon-as-shield entry: a sufficiently faster ship can escape this situation, so the two-ship problem is a serious threat rather than a guaranteed kill.

---

**Station keeping is cheap**
**[Certain]** Maintaining a stationary position above a surface at low altitude costs little enough fuel that it is not a meaningful tactical consideration. The raider is not bleeding its fuel reserves simply by sitting in orbit. The fuel endurance asymmetry applies to maneuvering and pursuit, not to station keeping.

---

**The moon's rotation**
**[Speculative]** Moons of this size are typically tidally locked to their parent body, rotating once per orbit. If Raiman's Folly follows this pattern, its rotation is very slow — far too slow for 30 kilometres to be anywhere near geostationary. Station keeping at that altitude against a slow rotation remains trivially cheap regardless.

## The ore launcher as a conditional weapon 

**[Certain]** The settlement's ore launcher is a mass driver with no guidance capability — packages are dumb munitions once away. Against a maneuvering ship at 30km altitude it is not viable.

**[Certain]** At 30km, packages arrive fast enough to threaten a stationary or disabled ship. The ore launcher therefore has latent offensive relevance if the raider vessel is disabled or unable to maneuver.

**[Speculative]** Whether a disabled raider at 30km altitude could be engaged before it recovered maneuvering capability depends on how quickly packages reach altitude and how the crew responds. Neither has been defined precisely enough to call.
