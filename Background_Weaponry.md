# Weapon Systems of the Forge

## Overview

The Forge's industrial constraints shape its weapons as much as everything else. The scientific knowledge to build sophisticated weapons exists. The manufacturing capacity to produce them at scale does not. What the Forge has is a weapons ecosystem built from what can actually be made, maintained, and sourced — functional, dangerous, and uneven in distribution.

---

## Ward Launchers and Ward Charges

The standard point defence system on armed civilian vessels. A ward launcher fires ward charges — munitions designed not to destroy incoming objects but to deflect them. A ward charge imparts enough force to push an asteroid, piece of debris, or dumb projectile off its course, causing it to miss rather than hit.

Ward systems exist primarily because ships and settlements in the Forge need protection against the mundane hazard of moving through space near rocks. Their secondary function as a weapons defence system is real but limited: ward charges are effective against anything that cannot correct its own course, and ineffective against anything that can. A guided missile simply adjusts after the push and continues toward its target.

Ward launchers are cheap and easy to produce and maintain, which is another reason lasers and larger kinetics are not generally used as point defense.

---

## Lasers

The standard ranged weapon of armed ships in the Forge. Lasers travel at lightspeed, which means at any combat-relevant range there is no dodging a shot once fired. What limits their effectiveness is power falloff over distance — the energy delivered to a target decreases significantly as range increases. Maximum effective range is about 2000 kilometers.

At long range, even a well-armed civilian ship can only harass and degrade. It can cook sensors, pit hull plating, and slowly accumulate damage on a ship that holds still — but it cannot precision-target a specific component or guarantee a kill. A navy vessel with proper fire control systems can be genuinely surgical at the same ranges, knocking out specific systems with accuracy a civilian cannot match.

This means a ship cannot simply park at what feels like a safe distance and wait. Laser harassment is a slow clock — not immediately lethal, but a persistent drain on hull integrity and systems that demands either movement or response. Ships under harassment must keep manoeuvering, which costs fuel and crew attention.

Lasers are main guns. They are long-range offensive and deterrent weapons, not point defence tools. Using your laser cannons to swat incoming objects is like using a sniper rifle to defend a doorway — possible, but not what the weapon is for.

## Kinetics

Chemically propelled projectile weapons. In vacuum, a kinetic round travels until it hits something — range is theoretically unlimited, but accuracy degrades over distance and anything with time to manoeuvre can avoid shots fired from far enough away. Deflecting shots with ward launchers is also an option if there's enough time to calculate the shot and launch the charge.

At 500 kilometres, most kinetic shots can be dodged or warded against by an alert crew with a manoeuvering ship. At 300 kilometres, most shots connect. Inside that envelope kinetics become brutal and decisive — high damage, fast rate of fire, no power falloff. They are the close-range finishing weapon, the tool for when ships are nearly on top of each other or when you are shooting at something that cannot move.

Settlements cannot move, but ward launchers can still provide meaningful defence against kinetic bombardment — provided the attacker is far enough away that there is time to calculate incoming trajectories and launch charges before the rounds arrive. At close range this ceases to be viable. The rounds arrive faster than effective counterfire can be organised.

---

## Missiles

The most dangerous weapons in common circulation in the Forge, and the rarest. Guided missiles carry meaningful warheads to their targets at range, correct course in flight, and cannot be defeated by ward charges. A missile that connects with a ship that is not built to absorb the hit will cripple or destroy it. They can essentially hit anything that the ship carrying them can target. Range is effectively limited by the missile's fuel, since once that runs out it's just a dumb munition. In addition, at longer ranges an opponent will have so many opportunities to hit the missile with defensive laser fire that the chances of destroying it in time go up. For a fast ship, five thousand kilometers should be a safe distance.

The industrial constraints of the Forge make missiles expensive to manufacture and impossible to source locally outside of established navy supply chains. A ship arrives with however many missiles it could acquire before departure, and that number does not increase in the field. Every shot is a strategic decision.

A ship carrying missiles has a deterrent entirely disproportionate to everything else about it. A harrier — faster, more manoeuverable, capable of laser harassment — cannot safely approach a vessel with missiles in its magazine, because one or two hits will end the engagement permanently.

Point defence against missiles is possible in principle — a laser cannon can burn down an incoming missile if targeting is good enough — but this is difficult, unreliable, and requires using your main guns reactively rather than offensively. It is not a counter so much as a last resort.

---

## Navy Weapons

Navy factions, particularly the larger cordon fleets, have access to weapons beyond what civilian vessels typically carry. Railguns — electromagnetic kinetic accelerators capable of firing dense projectiles at a significant fraction of lightspeed — exist in navy arsenals. The precision manufacturing required to produce them is beyond any current civilian industrial base. A navy vessel with railguns has a kinetic weapon that combines the range advantages of a laser with the mass and impact of a kinetic round. Encounters between large navy warships and anything else are consequently not contests.

Navy fire control systems also make the laser accuracy gap concrete and decisive. What a civilian combatant does at long range with lasers — harass and degrade — a navy vessel does with surgical precision. The institutional and technological gap between the navy factions and everyone else is nowhere more visible than in a direct engagement.
