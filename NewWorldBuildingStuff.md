## The Four Regions

**The Blight**
The quarantine zone enforced by the cordon navies. Contains two distinct categories of world:

- **The Old Worlds** — the planets humanity actually built its civilisation on. Cities, industry, shipyards, factory complexes, precision manufacturing infrastructure. All of it still there, still potentially functional, completely unreachable by surface. The most valuable real estate in human space, sitting behind a quarantine line enforced by the faction that benefits most from controlling access to it. The tension between the cordon navy's genuine protective mission and their de facto monopoly on whatever the Old Worlds contain is one of the setting's live political fault lines.

- **The Dead Worlds** — planets people fled to during the exodus, unknowingly carrying the blight with them. A second wave of catastrophe following the first. These worlds have human graves on them, and the ruins of settlements that almost made it. They are a particular kind of tragedy — the blight didn't find these people, they brought it themselves. Culturally this may inform a certain fatalism or extreme caution around contamination protocols.

The cordon navies patrol the boundary of the Blight, enforcing the quarantine against entry and exit alike. Their stated mission is preventing contamination from spreading outward. Their unstated strategic position is sole gatekeeping authority over the Old Worlds' intact industrial infrastructure.

---

**The Shell**
The region immediately surrounding the Blight — the destinations people reached during the panicked exodus before the navies concentrated survivors into the Refuge. Almost entirely depopulated now, but not empty. Contains two categories of location:

- **Ghost Colonies** — settlements that were abandoned, either because they were unviable, because the navies relocated their populations to the Refuge, or because something went wrong. Range from orderly evacuations where everything useful was stripped out, to places that were simply left mid-occupation. A century of abandonment means anything could still be running — automated systems, AI, infrastructure with no one to oversee it. Ghost Colonies are ready-made exploration locations. Not all of them are entirely dead. Some may have been quietly reoccupied.

- **Rogue Colonies** — settlements that refused navy authority. They don't pay taxes, don't accept navy jurisdiction, and don't receive navy protection. They are not actively hunted — the navies have bigger concerns and making martyrs of independent settlers is bad politics. But they exist entirely outside the systems of mutual obligation that make life in the Refuge function. Some are genuinely free and self-sufficient communities that made a principled choice. Others are desperate, preyed upon, and surviving on the margin. The interesting ones are probably somewhere between. Rogue Colonies may have found and use Old World technology and unaligned AI systems, having built relationships with whatever they found running in the Shell.

The Shell is also where unaligned AI is most likely to be encountered. Old World technology was running when colonies were abandoned. A century later some of it still is, doing whatever it was designed to do with no oversight and no off switch. An agricultural AI optimising for a population that no longer exists. A defence system still on alert. A mining operation that has been autonomously expanding for a hundred years. These aren't necessarily malevolent — just persistent, unchecked, and potentially very strange to interact with.

---

**The Refuge**
Where almost all humans live. The product of deliberate navy action in the first generation after the exodus — navies identified which shell settlements were viable, relocated populations from those that weren't, and concentrated humanity in a region with the most promise. This is the society the existing background documents describe.

The concentration process has a direct bearing on the planetary navy factions: a navy that transported a population to the Refuge and settled them has a natural claim to authority over that population, and those populations have a complex relationship with the navy that saved them and subsequently rules them — genuine gratitude layered over genuine resentment of the power asymmetry.

The Refuge is not a single contiguous region in any simple geographic sense. Its shape is determined by passage corridor availability rather than raw distance — FTL travel follows natural spatial phenomena, so two worlds physically close might be navigationally isolated while two distant worlds are well connected. The actual inhabited zone is a series of clusters and corridors rather than a uniform sphere, with the cordon navy's territory forming its inner boundary against the Shell and Blight.

---

**Deep Space**
Not a named region. Just the rest of space — the vast majority of it — where nobody lives and nothing is going on as far as anyone knows. Precursor sites exist out here as well as in the other regions. It has no identity as a place. It is simply the dark between things that matter.

## The Concentration History

In the first generation after the exodus, the shell was a spray of desperate settlements — chaotic, thin, most of them unviable. The navies, as the only organisations that maintained institutional coherence through the catastrophe, undertook a systematic triage: identifying which settlements could sustain themselves, which couldn't, and moving people accordingly. This was not universally welcomed. Some communities refused relocation and became the first Rogue Colonies. Some couldn't be reached in time. Some were already gone.

The concentration process shaped the Refuge's political geography in ways still felt a century later. The Refuge is not a neutral space where populations arrived independently — it is a space where populations were *placed*, by navies that then stayed. The planetary navy factions' authority over their populations has this history underneath it: we brought you here, we kept you alive, we built this. The civilian populations' resentment has the same history underneath it: we didn't choose this, we didn't choose you, and a century later you're still collecting the debt.

This history is old enough that most people alive now know it only as history. But it is recent enough — three or four generations — that it hasn't become myth. People's grandparents lived it. The institutional memory is still alive on both sides.
