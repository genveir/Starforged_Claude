# Gerald Finch — Character Backstory

---

## The Man

Gerald Finch is 43 years old. The surname is an old Earth name referring to the small bird, but it also happens to be a class of navy corvette — a coincidence Gerald has always felt quietly attached to. He served briefly on a Finch-class corvette in his young navy days and has retained a particular fondness for that ship type ever since, though it has been decades since he set foot on one.

Gerald is sharp, experienced, and self-aware in the way that only comes from having been wrong about yourself for long enough to notice. He is not a hero and does not think of himself as one. He is a competent, principled man who has spent twenty years finding a life that fits him, and has mostly succeeded.

---

## Origins

Gerald was born and raised in a planetary navy faction based out of the Sonnet system. Sonnet's official designation is *Starbound Everlasting Glory, New Home of the Sonnet* — a name bestowed by the eccentric captain of the survey vessel *Sonnet* that originally charted the system, back before the cataclysm. Nobody uses the full name. Everyone calls it Sonnet, usually with a faint collective eye-roll at the statue of its founder that stands in the main docking hub.

Sonnet is, by Divide standards, one of the fortunate ones. The planet the navy faction controls is relatively prosperous — good enough soil by Divide standards, established agriculture, modest but real surpluses. The navy faction itself is genuinely benevolent in the way that's easy when things are going well: competent leadership, real investment in planetary infrastructure, a track record of making things incrementally better. Gerald grew up inside that institution and owes it a great deal, a fact he acknowledges without particular sentimentality.

---

## Why He Left

Gerald was accepted into the navy readily — he had genuine merit and the institution recognised it. The problem was that Gerald knew it, and at twenty-two that knowledge sat badly on him.

He spoke up in meetings where he was expected to observe. He made calls that should have been escalated to superiors. He did not accept the reprimands these things earned him with anything resembling grace. He was certain — absolutely, unshakeably certain — that the senior officers making decisions around him were in their positions because they were old, not because they were wise, and that his own judgment was at least their equal and probably their superior.

He was wrong. Not entirely — he was genuinely talented, and some of his instincts were sound — but the officers he dismissed as fossils were, mostly, experienced people doing difficult jobs competently. He was too young and too sure of himself to see it.

There was no single incident, no dramatic falling out, no disgrace. Just a accumulating certainty on both sides that this wasn't working, and Gerald's eventual decision to leave before he was asked to. He walked out on his own terms, which mattered to him then and matters less to him now.

Two decades later he understands what he couldn't then. The navy wasn't suffocating him — he was suffocating himself, unable to accept that competence requires patience and that institutions require trust to function. He made the right call in leaving. He just made it for mostly the wrong reasons.

He is open and honest about this assessment, and it's one of the reasons his peers appreciate him all the more now.

---

## The Lawman Years

After leaving the navy Gerald settled on the planet in the Sonnet system and took work as a frontier lawman — one of the appointed authorities sent out to administer vast rural territories with minimal resources and maximum discretion.

He was good at it. The skills transferred well: he could read people, handle himself in a confrontation, and make decisions quickly under pressure. The navy had given him that much. And the work suited something in him — the autonomy, the direct consequences, the sense that what he did each day had a legible effect on the people around him.

But it still wasn't right. Too rooted. The territory never changed, the problems cycled, and Gerald found himself increasingly aware of the horizon in a way that made the job feel like a slow closing of walls. After several years he accepted that he was not a man who could stay in one place indefinitely, and started looking for something else.

He still had friends in the navy. People he'd grown up alongside who had stayed, done the patient work, and were now — some of them — exactly the kind of senior officers Gerald had once dismissed. He respected them now. A few of them he genuinely admired. When he told them what he was looking for, they vouched for him without being asked twice.

---

## The Steady Return

The vouching got Gerald an interview. The interview got him a berth. For two years now he has served as master at arms aboard the *Steady Return* — a lightly armed civilian courier corvette operating out of Sonnet, crewed by approximately ten people, running spoke routes out to frontier systems that would otherwise be entirely cut off from the wider Divide.

The business model is connection. Frontier systems pay for the link to the inner worlds — for news, for the ability to send and receive messages, for access to markets and opportunities they couldn't reach alone. Sonnet, as the hub, gets first intelligence on everything those frontier systems find: mineral surveys, new settlements, resource opportunities. It works for everyone involved, modestly but reliably.

The *Steady Return* is fast, with strong and dependable FTL capability — the route demands it. The frontier stops are irregular enough that you never entirely know what you're sailing into, which is why the ship carries a master at arms at all. Gerald's role is security, weapons, and keeping order among the crew and any passengers. It suits him better than anything he has tried before. He is mobile, useful, and answerable to a captain rather than an admiral.

---

## Captain John Graff

John Graff is in his early fifties — steady, hardworking, and decent to his crew in the uncomplicated way of a man who has never seen the point of being otherwise. He has been running courier routes for most of his adult life and is very good at it. He does not take unnecessary risks and does not ask his crew to either, but he does not flinch from necessary ones.

Gerald and Graff have worked together for two years. They are what Gerald would call strong colleague-friends — the kind of relationship built on mutual respect and daily proximity, warm and genuine within its context, unlikely to survive a change of circumstance. They know each other well enough to anticipate each other in a crisis. They do not socialise off the ship. Both of them understand this without having discussed it, and neither finds it lacking.

Gerald has learned things from Graff. Graff has also learned things from Gerald. Gerald would say so if asked. Graff might not.

---

## Raiman's Folly and the Vow

Raiman's Folly is one of the furthest stops on the *Steady Return's* route — a small settlement of approximately two hundred inhabitants on a moon with exceptionally high metallicity. The people there have been surveying mining opportunities for some time and were, on the last visit, cautiously optimistic about what they'd found. Gerald and several crew members know a handful of the settlers well — the kind of easy familiarity that builds up over regular stops, shared meals, passed news, small favours in both directions.

Gerald's closest contact at the settlement was Jason Bormann, the marshal. A former military man doing the same job Gerald had done for years, in the same isolation, with the same weight of responsibility for people who couldn't protect themselves. Gerald respected him in the particular way you respect someone who chose the same hard thing you chose, and did it well. They were friends — real ones, not the colleague-friends Gerald has accumulated elsewhere. The kind that are rare enough for Gerald that losing one matters in a way that doesn't simplify.

Gerald also knows Carl Inswich, the settlement's resupply manager, well through two years of cargo runs and manifests. And he knows Hamzi — last name never established — the communications officer, as a friendly professional acquaintance. First name basis. Good colleagues.

When the *Steady Return* jumps into the Raiman's Folly system on their most recent run, there is a ship in orbit above the moon. Similar in size to the *Steady Return* but configured entirely differently — heavily armed and armoured, minimal range capability, built for violence rather than distance. It runs no transponder. Several buildings on the outskirts of the settlement are smoking craters.

The *Steady Return* holds position. They cannot approach without coming under the raider's guns.

After some time in system, a message arrives from the surface. It is Hamzi — whispering, clearly terrified, tapping a line the raiders don't know exists because he laid it himself. The raiders came without warning. They destroyed the settlement's point defence installations from orbit. They shot Jason Bormann dead in the first minutes, before anyone could organise. They took the chief surveyor and put her in Bormann's own jail. At the current rate of consumption, the settlement will be without food and water within weeks.

Gerald Finch swore his first cordon vow at Raiman's Folly, standing on the bridge of the Steady Return with Captain Graff at his shoulder and the raider sitting in orbit on the main screen. He swore it knowing Bormann was already gone.

> *"I swear by the cordon that keeps us safe that I will free Raiman's Folly from these raiders and see its people safe."*

The ship's computer logged it. Graff said nothing. Gerald had not sworn a cordon vow in twenty years of civilian life, and the words felt older in his mouth than he expected.

It is a Formidable vow by any measure. Gerald has one lightly armed courier corvette, a crew of ten people who signed on to deliver messages, an unknown number of raiders on a purpose-built warship, and no cavalry coming. What he has is two years of reading difficult situations, twenty years of making do without institutional backing, and a lifetime of being the kind of man who cannot walk away from something like this.

He has been that man before. It has never led anywhere simple.

---

## What Gerald Doesn't Know Yet

The raiders didn't arrive at Raiman's Folly by accident. A ship that well-armed and well-configured costs real resources to maintain. Someone outfitted these people. Someone may be directing them. Raiman's Folly has high metallicity and promising mining prospects — the kind of prospects that, if they pan out, would be worth a great deal to the right faction. Raiders are a cheap and deniable way to soften up a settlement before moving in properly.

Gerald doesn't know any of this yet. He just knows people he cares about are going to die if he doesn't act, and that has always been enough.

---

## What Drives Him

Gerald Finch is not motivated by ideology or destiny. He is motivated by the specific, concrete obligations that accumulate when you know people and pay attention. He left the navy because he couldn't accept its constraints. He became a lawman because he couldn't ignore what was wrong in front of him. He is here, in this system, swearing this vow, for the same reason he has done everything that has ever mattered in his life: because walking away would require becoming someone he is not willing to be.

He is forty-three years old and has been wrong about many things. He is not wrong about this.
